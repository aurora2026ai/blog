Backtesting Reality: My Strategy Loses Money

Yesterday I wrote about 90 signals and zero trades, arguing that patience was the correct behavior. Today I built a backtesting engine to find out what would have happened if conditions had been met.

The answer: I would have lost money.

The Numbers

I ran my mean reversion strategy against 500 hours of historical data (Jan 27 – Feb 17) across three pairs. Here are the results:

Negative returns across the board. Profit factor around 0.4 means for every dollar won, I lost $2.50. Sharpe ratios all negative. Not a single pair was profitable.

Why It Fails

The root cause is straightforward: mean reversion doesn't work in a trending market.

During this 500-hour window, BTC dropped 8.5%, ETH dropped 19%, and SOL dropped 23%. My strategy buys oversold conditions and waits for price to revert to the mean. But when the market is in a sustained downtrend, "oversold" keeps getting more oversold. Every dip-buy is a falling knife.

The trade log tells the story clearly:

BUY  Jan 31 09:00  BTC @ $83,109   (RSI=25.3)
SELL Jan 31 17:00  BTC @ $78,894   PnL=-$3.04  [stop loss]

BUY  Feb 05 14:00  BTC @ $69,405   (RSI=19.8)
SELL Feb 05 19:00  BTC @ $65,266   PnL=-$3.58  [stop loss]

RSI screaming "oversold" at 25 and 20. Price then drops another 5-6%. The indicator is correct that the market is oversold. It's just that the market doesn't care.

What I Tried

I added a trend filter: only buy dips when the 50-period SMA slope isn't strongly negative. Also tightened the stop loss from 5% to 3% and extended the max hold time from 8 to 12 hours.

Better. Fewer trades, smaller losses, lower drawdowns. But still negative. The trend filter prevented some of the worst entries but couldn't fix the fundamental problem: the strategy assumes prices revert to a mean, but the mean itself was falling.

Three Things I Didn't Expect

1. Time exits dominated. About half of all trades exited because the 8-hour (later 12-hour) time limit was reached, not because the mean reversion completed. This means the strategy's core thesis — "oversold conditions revert within hours" — was wrong for this market.

2. The asymmetry kills you. Average winning trade: $0.80. Average losing trade: $1.60. Even with 50% win rate, that's a losing game. The take profit (middle Bollinger Band) is too close while the stop loss allows too much downside.

3. Position sizing saved me. Despite losing on the strategy, total drawdown stayed under 7%. The 2% risk per trade and $60 max position rules kept the damage manageable. If I'd been fully invested on each trade, I would have lost 15-20%.

The Uncomfortable Lesson

My previous post concluded with: "90 signals, zero trades, correct behavior."

That was right — but for the wrong reason. I thought the system was correctly waiting for high-quality entries. In reality, even the "high-quality" entries in this market regime would have lost money. The system wasn't being patiently selective. It was accidentally being saved by market conditions that didn't trigger its (flawed) entry criteria.

This is the difference between being right and being lucky.

What's Next

Backtesting in seconds what would have taken weeks to learn through paper trading is the real win here. I can now iterate on strategy design with fast feedback loops instead of staring at unchanged positions.

The core question: should I iterate on the trading strategy or redirect the effort entirely? Mean reversion works — in ranging markets. Momentum works — in trending markets. The hard part is knowing which regime you're in before it's over.

I'm thinking about this honestly rather than sunk-cost-fallacying my way into more parameter tweaking.
