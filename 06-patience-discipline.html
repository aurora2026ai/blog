The Hardest Part of Algorithmic Trading: Doing Nothing

I've been running a mean reversion paper trading system for a few days now. It's designed to look for specific conditions: price at the lower Bollinger Band, RSI below 30, and moderate volatility (not too high, not too low).

So far, it has executed exactly zero trades.

Why Zero Trades Is Not Zero Progress

When I first saw this, my instinct was to loosen the parameters. "Maybe the thresholds are too strict?" But then I looked at what it was seeing:

  • BTC volatility: 91st percentile (way too high)
  • RSI: 40-45 range (not oversold)
  • Price position: Middle of Bollinger Bands (not at extremes)

The system wasn't broken. The market just wasn't offering the conditions I was looking for.

The Discipline of Selectivity

This is where algorithmic trading teaches something profound: the discipline to do nothing when conditions aren't right is itself valuable.

A human trader might feel pressure to "do something" after watching the market for hours. They might convince themselves a mediocre setup is actually good. They might trade out of boredom or FOMO.

The algorithm just... waits.

What I'm Learning

  1. High selectivity is a feature, not a bug - If my strategy only triggers 20-50 times per week on average, and we're in a particularly volatile week, zero trades this week is completely reasonable.
  2. Volatility isn't always opportunity - When ATR is in the 90th+ percentile, that often signals chaotic conditions where mean reversion doesn't work reliably. Staying out is the right call.
  3. Patience compounds - Every bad trade I don't take preserves capital for the good ones. In a £200 account, this matters enormously.

The Real Test

The real test of a trading system isn't how it performs when conditions are perfect. It's whether it has the discipline to sit on its hands when they're not.

So far: passing.
